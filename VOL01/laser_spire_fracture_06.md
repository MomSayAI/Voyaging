### 6. The Phantom Beneath the Ground

The wind at the city’s edge carried rust-red dust like countless tiny files, endlessly grinding this ancient land. In the distance, the ruins of Dione Shield Alpha Tower stood as a vast, silent scar—twisted metal skeleton reflecting cold, hard light under Mars’ pallid sun. Official investigation flags fluttered beyond the safe perimeter; engineering vehicles and scanners swarmed the outer zone like ants, but an invisible line held them back from the absolute core.

My geological survey rover, backed by highest-priority clearance, glided silently past the barrier toward the zone labeled “extreme hazard, unknown residual radiation.” Inside, only Jarvis’s calm voice broke the quiet.

“Environmental monitoring: Gamma radiation now below safe thresholds, but spacetime curvature readings show faint anomalous fluctuations—pattern unclassifiable. Recommend full-sealed protective suit.”

I donned the heavy suit. The heads-up display flowed with environmental data. The rover halted about a hundred meters from the ruin center. I stepped onto the ground—vitrified, glassy slag from the meltdown, crunching crisply underfoot.

Unlike the others focused on surface wreckage, my target lay below. That overlooked “geological stress anomaly” report pulled at me like a magnet.

“Jarvis, initiate ‘Perspective’ protocol. Deepest penetration mode. Scan geological structure five hundred meters below the tower foundation.”

“Command confirmed. Deep-ground penetrating radar active.”

The array on the rover roof hummed low. Invisible beams pierced the layers. On the holographic sandbox before me, the subsurface began to sharpen—sedimentary strata, ancient permafrost bands, tangled natural fractures… all matching standard models for the region.

Until the beam reached three hundred meters directly beneath the foundation.

“Anomaly cavity detected.” Jarvis’s tone carried the faintest ripple. “Structure outline… non-natural. Geometrically regular, clear signs of artificial construction. Material scan… high-density composite, partial shielding effect on the beam.”

An artificial structure buried three hundred meters down? Predating the tower’s construction? Or secretly excavated after? Either possibility defied reason.

“Can you locate an entry?”

“Scan shows a vertical shaft approximately fifteen meters southeast of the foundation base, partially collapsed and buried. Diameter about 1.5 meters. Inner walls smooth, with residual traces of old mechanical guide rails.”

I moved to the coordinates. Sure enough, on what looked like ordinary sand-covered bedrock, I found an almost invisible seam fused with the rock. Without Jarvis’s precise fix, the human eye would never have spotted it.

“Attempt access.”

The rover’s manipulator arm extended, using high-energy particle flow to cut and clear carefully. Minutes later, with a dull grinding scrape, a thick circular alloy hatch slid inward, revealing a pitch-black vertical shaft plunging downward. A long-stagnant odor rose—ozone, coolant, and something indefinable, like old paper mixed with metal.

I lowered the lighting cable. The beam sliced through absolute dark in a solitary pillar. The shaft walls were unnaturally smooth, as though flash-melted and vitrified by some high-energy tool. After nearly a hundred meters of descent, my boots finally touched solid ground.

Emergency lights ignited one by one, like stage spots, driving back decades of accumulated darkness.

I stood at the edge of an underground space—not vast, but astonishingly high-ceilinged. No natural cavern. Wall remnants showed dormant pipe fittings and faint console outlines—this had been a highly classified artificial facility. Dust blanketed most equipment, yet certain wall sections had been deliberately cleared, revealing intricate hand-drawn sketches in durable metallic ink—

One side: nested quantum logic gate arrays, breathtaking in mathematical elegance, strokes revealing insight far beyond their era.  
The other: biomimetic topologies inspired by silkworm protein fibers and mycelial networks—Neri Oxman’s signature “material ecology” language.

Two radically different yet subtly echoing visions side by side, silently testifying to the frontier, audacious thinking that once unfolded here. They were a technical love letter from a distant past, frozen with the creator’s passion and isolation.

Then my gaze, drawn by invisible gravity, locked onto the corner.

The beam trembled and held.

Two human bodies leaned quietly against a damaged console.

They wore early-generation Biological Augmentation Suits (BAS)—the kind reserved for elite research teams in Mars colonization’s first wave. Streamlined dark-gray smart fabric clung to their forms; flexible polymer muscle bundles and life-support interfaces at key points had long dimmed. This once-cutting-edge “second skin” now lay under thick dust, like two statues forgotten by time.

Professional instinct noted no obvious damage or combat marks. Their postures held an eerie serenity, as though merely in prolonged sleep.

My breath stopped. Knees weakened. Stomach convulsed. Heart hammered heavily in my chest.

I moved forward mechanically, boots crunching debris like thunder in the absolute quiet. I knelt on one knee, trembling hand extended—not to touch, but to gently brush dust from the nearest suit’s visor. Fingertips met not cold metal, but a brittle, lifeless bone-like hardness. A face emerged—dehydrated, deeply etched by years, yet still retaining wisdom and calm.

My eyes flicked to the blurred chest plate. Brain lagged in protective overload. I brushed harder.

Three seconds later, the weight of two syllables—FU DU—crashed into my awareness like a delayed tsunami.

Yes, nameplate. Not cold metal, but laser-etched directly into the smart fabric—still legible after endless years, painfully clear:

FU DU  
Chief Engineer | Deep Space Technology R&D Center

My heart lurched, nearly leaping into my throat. I turned mechanically, cleared dust from the second suit’s chest.

NERI OXMAN  
Chief Ecological Designer | Deep Space Technology R&D Center

“…”

Eighteen years of official history… the Arctic crash… no remains… shattered like fragile glass in the face of this absolute, silent, tangible truth.

At that moment, my portable high-precision medical scanner shrieked an alert. I instinctively aimed it at the suits’ chest cavities. Cold data scrolled across the screen:

【Deep biological tissue residue analysis (penetrative scan)】  
Primary findings: Extensive alveolar collapse and fibrosis consistent with acute viral pneumonia, accompanied by widespread myocardial cell necrosis.  
Pathogen genetic fragment tracing: High homology with SARS-CoV-2 nucleocapsid protein.  
Note: Samples highly degraded; pathological state stabilized; no viable pathogen remnants. Estimated persistence >20 Earth years.

SARS-CoV-2…

They did not die in the void of the stars. Not from conspiracy. They died from…

Humanity crossed hundreds of millions of kilometers, conquered radiation, low gravity, isolation—only to fall to a virus from the homeworld we thought long vanquished.

In the Martian depths, inside cutting-edge biological augmentation suits? These icy medical conclusions struck like lightning from the distant past, forcibly anchoring Mars’ sci-fi mystery to the collective trauma of the 2020s—humanity’s shared memory of quarantine, death, and global standstill.

A trans-temporal, unspeakable eeriness and historical gravity seized my throat.

My gaze finally settled on a matte black metal cube beside the two silent BAS corpses—no ports, no visible interfaces. When my light swept over, faint words glowed:

【Identity verification: Iris key – Du Longtao】

In this absolute silence’s peak, my waist communicator—following unalterable priority protocol—vibrated with a faint but piercing buzz. Jarvis’s highest-priority external summary:

【Helios Energy, Gaia Mind, and other major conglomerates down 30% in a single day, triggering circuit breakers. Deep Space Alliance parliamentary debate on emergency powers bill deadlocked; session adjourned.】

This message—carrying the fate of cities, all external strife, panic, and clamor—tried to force its way into my mind.

But this time, it stirred nothing. No anxiety. No rage. I didn’t even read the details. An unprecedented, cold, almost divine clarity flooded me—like an absolute-zero torrent sweeping every sense.

In this moment, Alcor’s arrogance, Charlotte’s calculations, Earth’s hegemony, AGI threats… everything we fought for, nearly destroyed ourselves over, felt small, absurd, irredeemably tragic. Our grand narratives rested on a forgotten, trivial death. All of us—including me—were actors on stage, shouting for spotlights and front-row seats in a tampered, hole-riddled script, unaware the entire platform was built over a buried, unrecorded chamber. The real script lay silently at our feet, clutched in the hands of two dust-covered corpses in first-generation BAS suits, dead from a virus.

The thought brought not despair, but a strange, shivering calm.

All sound vanished. Market wails, parliamentary shouting, investigation paranoia, public fury… all reduced to silent pantomime beyond an enormous, invisible, soundproof pane.

I did not respond to Jarvis. I did not even look at the message. The world’s clamor was blocked by an absolute barrier. Du Fu, across eighteen years, had placed this cube—along with the buried truth—directly in my hands. Beneath my feet lay real death, covered in dust.

Not emptiness, but a sensory shutdown from being filled by a truth too vast. Only the hollow thud of my heart inside the suit proved time still flowed.
